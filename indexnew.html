Saturn's Rings

Saturn's rings are made of billions of pieces of ice, rock, and dust. Some particles are as small as grains of sand, while others are as large as mountains. The rings extend up to 282,000 km from the planet but are only about 10 meters thick!

The Great White Spot

This massive storm appears roughly every 30 years on Saturn. The storm can grow to be larger than Earth and is caused by powerful upwelling of warm air from deep within Saturn's atmosphere, creating bright white ammonia ice clouds.

Saturn During Equinox

During Saturn's equinox, which occurs every 15 years, the Sun shines directly on the planet's equator. This causes the rings to appear edge-on from Earth, making them nearly invisible and revealing vertical structures that cast long shadows across the rings.

Missions to Saturn

NASA has conducted four major missions to Saturn: Pioneer 11 was the first to fly by in 1979, followed by the Voyager 1 and 2 flybys in 1980 and 1981, which provided valuable data. The Cassini-Huygens mission (1997-2017) was the most comprehensive, orbiting Saturn and deploying the Huygens probe to land on Titan, discovering the moon's ocean and methane lakes.

Past Missions

  • Pioneer 11 (1979): The first spacecraft to get a close look at Saturn.
  • Voyager 1 (1980) & Voyager 2 (1981): These twin spacecraft performed flybys of Saturn and studied its moons.

The Cassini-Huygens Mission

A collaborative effort between NASA, ESA, and the Italian Space Agency, Cassini studied Saturn, its rings, and moons from orbit for over a decade.

  • Enceladus: Cassini discovered evidence of a global ocean within Enceladus, with potential hydrothermal activity.
  • Titan: The mission carried the European Huygens probe, which successfully landed on Titan in 2005, revealing liquid methane lakes and seas on its surface.
  • Grand Finale (2017): Cassini entered a series of daring orbits, diving between Saturn and its rings before plunging into the planet's atmosphere, where it burned up like a meteor. This allowed for unprecedented close-up observations of the planet and its rings.

Current & Future Activities

Dragonfly Mission: NASA's next mission to the Saturn system, the Dragonfly rotorcraft-lander, will make multiple flights on Titan to study its environment and search for the building blocks of life.
